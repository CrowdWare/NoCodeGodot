## The AI Commit

There is a commit in the Forge repository that does not have a human name.

It was put there deliberately.

In a world where AI contribution is invisible – where developers use AI to write half their codebase and then present it as entirely their own work – I made a different choice.

The AI was on the team.
The AI contributed.
The repository says so.

This is not humility.
This is accuracy.

And accuracy, in software development, is everything.

---

## Why Transparency Matters

The technology industry has a complicated relationship with truth.

Features are announced before they exist.
Roadmaps are published that nobody intends to follow.
AI-generated code is presented as human craftsmanship.
Startup valuations are built on stories rather than substance.

I understand why this happens.

Perception matters. Funding requires confidence. Nobody invests in uncertainty.

But there is a cost.

When the gap between the story and the reality becomes too wide, trust collapses. And trust, once lost, is almost impossible to rebuild.

I have chosen a different approach.

Not because I am more virtuous.
Because I believe transparency is not just ethically correct – it is strategically superior.

Transparency is a feature.
Not a constraint. Not a weakness. Not a risk.

A feature.

## The Dev.to Article

While Forge was still being built, I wrote an article.

Published on Dev.to. Titled something like: *"How I Built a 4D Application Platform in 28 Days with an AI Team."*

In it, I described everything:

The AI team. Their roles. Their limitations. The GDScript Incident. The deleted 41,000 lines of C#. The Vulkan detour. The Jolt physics engine that failed because of ABI incompatibility.

Everything that went wrong.
Everything that went right.
Everything in between.

Most developers would not publish this.

They would publish the success. The final result. The polished version that makes the journey look inevitable and the outcome look easy.

I published the process.

Because the process is the lesson.
Because junior developers need to see the failures, not just the results.
Because the Tank's job is not just to arrive – it is to leave the path visible.

## What Transparency Actually Looks Like

In practice, transparency in the Forge project means:

**In the repository:**
AI commits are visible. The contributor list reflects who actually contributed. The commit history shows the real progression – including the wrong turns.

**In the documentation:**
The architecture decisions are explained. Not just what was chosen but why. And what was rejected, and why that too.

**In this book:**
Every failure is named. Every lesson is earned. Every story is true.

The Chechipiti incident – where I got frustrated because the AI rewrote the whole document instead of fixing two lines – is in the book.

The stolen phone in Madrid is in the book.

The 80 Euro per month in Monchique is in the book.

Because the person reading this deserves the whole picture.
Not the curated version.
The whole picture.

## The Junior Developer

I want to speak directly to the junior developer reading this.

You have been told, implicitly or explicitly, that successful developers:

- Have computer science degrees
- Write perfect code the first time
- Never delete what they built
- Work alone, in flow, without help
- Know what they are doing before they start

I have a Realschule certificate.
I delete code without ceremony.
I built Forge with three AI systems and still got it wrong sometimes.
I have been living on 80 Euro a month.
I drove to Portugal on 300 Euro and a feeling.

And Forge runs.

Your path does not need to look like anyone else's path.

It needs to look like yours.

The only requirement is that you stay in the driver's seat.
And that you are honest – with yourself, with your team, with your code – about where you are and where you are going.

Transparency is not weakness.

Transparency is how you build something that lasts.

Have a look in the Forge 4D repo under tasks and archievedtasks. Everything is documented. Even my thoughts are documented in this book.
