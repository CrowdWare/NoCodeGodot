## The GPS Metaphor

Here is how to work with AI without losing yourself in it.

You are the driver.
The AI is the GPS.

The GPS knows a lot. It knows every road. It can calculate routes faster than any human. It will get you there – usually.

But the GPS does not know your destination.

You tell the GPS where to go.
You follow the route – but you look out the window.
You notice when something is wrong.
You override when necessary.

You do not let the GPS drive.

This is the only rule you need for AI-assisted development:

**Stay in the driver's seat.**

The AI will fill in details. It will generate code. It will suggest approaches. It will sometimes do the wrong thing confidently and completely.

Your job is to know the destination well enough to recognize when the route is wrong.

That requires you to understand what you are building.
Not every line of code.
The destination.

## What You Actually Need

You do not need:
- A university degree
- The perfect development environment
- The latest MacBook
- A quiet office
- Five years of experience before you start

You need:
- Curiosity about how things work
- Willingness to be wrong and try again
- The ability to describe what you want clearly
- Enough understanding to recognize when the output is wrong
- Patience with the process

That is the complete list.

Everything else is learned by doing.

## The Rosinen Method

When I was training Leonid – the young Wirtschaftsinformatik student at Dresdner Bank – he read every book completely. Every word. Every footnote.

I told him:

*"Leonid. You don't need to read the whole book. Pick out the Rosinen. The important parts."*

He looked at me like I had suggested something slightly illegal.

But here is the truth:

Nobody has time to know everything.
Nobody needs to know everything.

You need to know:
- The shape of the problem
- The tools available
- How to find the specific information you need when you need it

The rest you look up.

This has always been true.
In the age of AI, it is more true than ever.

You do not need to memorize syntax.
You need to understand concepts.

The AI handles syntax.
You handle concepts.

## Don't Blame the Machine

I want to address something directly.

You will get frustrated with AI.

You will ask for something specific and receive something general.
You will ask for two lines to be fixed and receive the whole document rewritten.
You will lose context between sessions and watch the AI confidently build something that contradicts what you built yesterday.

When this happens – and it will happen – do not blame the machine.

The machine did exactly what the prompt said.
The machine filled the gaps with its best guess.
The machine had no way of knowing what you meant rather than what you said.

The responsibility is yours.

Not because you are inferior.
Because you are the human.
Because you know the destination.
Because you are the driver.

Better prompts produce better results.
Clearer context produces clearer output.
More specific instructions produce more specific code.

Every time the AI produces something wrong, ask yourself:

*What did I not say clearly enough?*

This question will make you a better developer faster than any course or certification.

## One Last Thing

You will fail.

Not maybe. Not if you are unlucky.

You will fail. Multiple times. In multiple ways.

The XML prototype at Dresdner Bank never became a product.
The Kotlin attempt failed.
The Vulkan detour failed.
41,000 lines of C# were deleted.
900 lines of GDScript had to be rewritten.

Every failure was a navigation tool.
Every dead end pointed somewhere.
Every Guru Meditation produced a reboot.

Failure is not the opposite of success.
Failure is the process through which success becomes possible.

Stay in the driver's seat.
Keep the destination clear.
Trust the pull before you understand it.
And when the machine writes GDScript instead of C++ –

Laugh.
Fix it.
Keep going.

You belong here.
