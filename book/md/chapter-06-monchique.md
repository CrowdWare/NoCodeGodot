# Chapter 6 – Monchique: 80 Euro, 60 Hectares, and the First Community

---

You don't need much to live.
You need much less than you think.
The system has been lying to you about this your entire life.

---

## The Pandemic Arrives

The world stopped in March 2020.

For most people this was a shock. A disruption. The sudden collapse of everything that had seemed permanent and reliable.

For me, it was a practical problem.

No more busking. The streets were empty. The gasse in Caldas da Rainha where I had played guitar every morning – silent. The fresh market – closed. The coins that had supplemented my 300 Euro – gone.

My mind started its familiar routine:

*Now you are really finished. No income. No plan. Stuck in a foreign country during a global pandemic with almost no money.*

My heart said: *something is coming. Wait.*

---

## Facebook and Domingos

While the world panicked, I posted on Facebook.

There was a group called Rainbow Lands Portugal. I had been a member for a while. By this point I was an administrator.

I wrote a simple post: I am looking for land. For a community. In Portugal. Does anyone know of something?

Domingos replied.

A Portuguese man from the Alentejo region – or further south, I am not entirely sure anymore, somewhere in the warm dry heartland of Portugal. He had land in Monchique, in the hills of the Algarve. Sixty hectares of it.

Sixty hectares.

For context: sixty hectares is roughly the size of eighty-four football fields. Forest, hills, streams, space. The kind of land that in Germany would cost a fortune and require seventeen permits and a committee.

In Portugal, in 2020, during a pandemic, it was available.

*Come,* Domingos said. *We can build something here.*

"Domingos had spent fifteen years in South Africa. He knew Michael Tellinger personally. And suddenly I understood exactly why I was there."

---

## Monchique

The hills of Monchique are not like the tourist Portugal of the Algarve coast.

No beach bars. No sunburned Germans on loungers. No postcards.

Just hills. Cork trees. The smell of eucalyptus and earth. Silence of a quality that cities have forgotten exists.

Añona my dog and me arrived – and we began.

Not with a plan. Not with funding. Not with a business model or a governance structure or a mission statement.

With hands. With time. With the simple understanding that if you show up every day and do the work, something grows.

We built the first community.

---

## 80 Euro a Month

Let me tell you how I lived.

Every month I received royalties from my first book. Approximately 50 - 80 Euro.

That was my income.

80 Euro per month.

Here is what 80 Euro bought in Monchique in 2020:

- 10 kilograms of rice
- 10 tins of cooked beans
- One bottle of olive oil
- A SIM card with data

Rice. Beans. Olive oil. Internet.

I had everything I needed.

Not everything the system says you need. Not the apartment and the car and the subscriptions and the coffee shop visits and the new phone every two years.

Everything I actually needed.

Food. Shelter – the land provided that. Community – the people provided that. Purpose – the work provided that. Connection – the SIM card provided that.

50 Euro for food and SIM card.
30 Euro remaining.

I was not poor.

I was free.

---

## What Money Actually Is

There is a story we are told from childhood.

The story goes: you need money to live. More money means better life. Less money means worse life. The goal is to accumulate as much as possible so that one day you will have enough.

Enough for what, exactly, is never specified.

In Monchique, on 80 Euro a month, I understood something that no amount of financial planning had ever taught me:

Money is not the resource. 

**Time is the resource.**
**Attention is the resource.**
**Community is the resource.**

Money is just one way of accessing those things. And often – very often – it is the most expensive and least efficient way.

On 60 hectares in the hills of Portugal, with rice and beans and olive oil and people who shared the same vision, I had more of what actually matters than I had during any well-paid Frankfurt contract.

This is not romanticization.
This is arithmetic.

---

## The Scout

During the pandemic, when people were afraid to leave their houses, we scouted land.

Domingos knew the region and had connections. I had energy and time and the by-now well-practiced ability to show up somewhere with nothing and find what was needed.

We drove through the Alentejo. Through the Serra de Monchique. Through valleys where the only sounds were water and birds and the occasional distant tractor.

We looked at land.

Some of it was wrong – too isolated, too expensive, too complicated.
Some of it was interesting.
Some of it was exactly what we were looking for.

We were not buying yet. We were learning. Building the map of what was possible. Understanding the landscape – literally and metaphorically.

This is also scouting.

Years later, when I dream of a sailboat moving between islands and coastlines, finding land for communities to grow – I recognize the shape of that dream.

It is the same work.
Different scale.
Same soul.

## Wien

And then came the invitation.

Vienna. Austria. A festival about Ubuntu – the philosophy, not the operating system. Though for me, increasingly, they were the same thing.

*I am because we are.*

They wanted me to speak.

About what, exactly? About living Ubuntu. About the community in Monchique. About what it actually looks like when people stop waiting for someone else to build the world they want to live in and simply begin building it.

I stood in front of an audience in Vienna – a Realschule graduate, a former bank contractor, a man who had recently been living on rice and beans in the hills of Portugal – and I said:

**"We don't need to wait for the people who will implement this. We are the people who will implement this."**

The room understood.

Not because I was eloquent. Not because I had impressive credentials.

Because it was true.

And truth, spoken clearly, lands differently than everything else.

---

## Bauchi and the New Earth

At that festival I met Bauchi for the first time.

Jesus Bruder Bauchi – a man who had written a book called *2020 Die Neue Erde.* A Regnose. A vision of the future written as if it had already happened, then working backwards to show how humanity arrived there.

Through his book, I learned how to write Regnose properly.

Not as prediction. Not as hope.
As memory of something that hasn't happened yet.

You place yourself in the future.
You look around.
You describe what you see.
You write it in the past tense.

*We built communities. We found land. We sailed between coastlines. We shared what we knew. We remembered what we had forgotten.*

It sounds simple.
It changes everything.

Because the mind can argue with a plan.
The mind cannot argue with a memory.

When you write the future as already real, something shifts. The question changes from *can this happen?* to *how did this happen?*

And the how always reveals itself.

It always has.

---

## The Shape of the Vision

Monchique gave me something I didn't know I was looking for.

Proof of concept.

Not for Forge. Not for software. For everything.

Proof that you can live on almost nothing and be genuinely, deeply rich.
Proof that community forms when people show up with shared intention.
Proof that the land provides when you treat it with respect.
Proof that Ubuntu is not a philosophy you discuss at festivals.

It is a practice.
It is a daily decision.
It is rice and beans and olive oil and sixty hectares and people who believe the same impossible things.

And it works.

I know it works because I lived it.

On 80 Euro a month.
In the hills of Monchique.
During a pandemic that the rest of the world experienced as catastrophe.

I experienced it as confirmation.
