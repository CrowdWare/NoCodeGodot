# Chapter 4 – Too Much, Too Soon
*Zu viel, zu früh*

---

There is a version of this story where I tell you about the burnout clinically.
Stress levels. Work-life balance. The importance of boundaries.

This is not that version.

---

## The Collapse

At some point I was holding three things at once:

- A full-time job at the bank
- Interaction Design studies
- Building RIAMS – a CMS and application platform on Silverlight and Telerik

Each one alone would have been enough. Together they were a wall I kept running into until I couldn't run anymore.

The burnout didn't arrive dramatically. It arrived quietly, like a system that simply stops responding. No error message. No warning. Just – nothing left.

The bank stopped making sense. Corporate work, corporate rhythms, corporate priorities. None of it was aligned with where I was trying to go. So I stopped.

And then I was standing in Berlin. Without a job. Without a plan. With a 30-year-old van I had bought for 4.000 € – inherited within a family, passed from hand to hand until it reached mine.

---

## The Library Years

Berlin has excellent public libraries.

I know this because for a period of time, the library was my office. Free WiFi. Power outlets. Warmth. A place where nobody asks why you're there all day with a laptop.

I went almost every day.

And I worked.

Not on a career. Not on a plan. On ideas. On code. On books. Because even when everything else had collapsed, the urge to build things had not.

This is something they don't tell you about burnout: it doesn't necessarily kill your creativity. Sometimes it just strips away everything that was suffocating it.

---

## The Road

Then came the pandemic. And paradoxically – the road opened up.

With a van and no fixed address, borders closing didn't mean the same thing it meant for everyone else. I was already mobile. I was already living differently.

I drove through Portugal. Through Spain. Toward Switzerland.

In Bern I sat in the university and wrote. Not as a student – just as someone who needed a table, a connection, and silence. Universities are generous with space if you look like you belong.

In Lisbon I sat in a café and worked on a book. The kind of café where nobody rushes you. Outside: the city. On the screen: ideas taking shape.

In Portugal I parked the 30-year-old van outside a university for two weeks. I ran an extension cable. I had a small fan for the heat. I worked inside the van, and when it got too hot, I worked inside the university.

No office. No contract. No address.

But: code was written. Books were finished. Ideas survived.

*(During this time I also wrote extensively across many topics – software development, spirituality, personal growth. See Appendix E for the full list.)*

---

## What This Period Actually Was

It would be easy to frame this as a low point. The world's smallest office. A borrowed van older than some programming languages. WLAN from a library card.

But that framing misses the truth.

This period was not a failure with creativity surviving despite the circumstances.
It was freedom – uncomfortable, unplanned, unglamorous freedom – in which the work continued because the work was never dependent on the circumstances.

The idea that became Forge didn't need a standing desk and a Spotify subscription.
It needed someone stubborn enough to keep going.

---

## The Parkbank

Eventually the van died. Portugal. Side of the road. End of the line for a 30-year-old engine that had carried everything it could carry.

I found myself on a park bench in Berlin.

No van. No office. No plan.

Just the same ideas that had been there at the bank, at RIAMS, in the library, in Bern, in Lisbon, in the Portuguese heat with a fan and an extension cable.

---

And now?

28 days. 41.000 lines of code. An AI team. A platform thirty years in the making.
