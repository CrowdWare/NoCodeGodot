## The Open Source Question

Forge is open source.

This is not an accident. It is not a marketing decision. It is a philosophical position:

Software that shapes how other software is built should be transparent.

SML files are plain text. Anyone can read them. Anyone can version them, diff them, archive them, understand them years later without asking permission or paying a license fee.

The Forge Runner is open source. The SML parser is open source. The SMS interpreter is open source.

Because the foundation must be trustworthy.
And trust requires visibility.

---

## The Dual License

But open source does not mean free for everyone in every context.

Forge uses a dual license model:

**GPL3 License – for personal, educational, and open source use.**

You are a student learning to build software. Free.
You are an indie developer building your own tool. Free.
You are a researcher experimenting with declarative UI systems. Free.
You are contributing back to the community. Free.

**Commercial License – for companies building products.**

You are a company integrating Forge into a commercial product. License required.
You are building a SaaS platform on top of Forge. License required.
You are using Forge to build software you sell. License required.

This is not greed.
This is sustainability.

Open source projects die when the people who build them cannot afford to continue. The dual license model ensures that the community gets full access while the companies that profit from the work contribute to its survival.

## CrowdWare DAO 

The commercial license fees do not go to a corporation.

They go to the CrowdWare DAO.

A Decentralized Autonomous Organization – governed by its members, transparent in its decisions, accountable to the community rather than to shareholders.

The Swiss Holding provides the legal structure.
The DAO provides the governance.
The community provides the direction.

Revenue from commercial licenses funds:
- Continued development of Forge
- Land acquisition for Ubuntu communities
- ForgeSTA development for Android
- The CrowdBook platform
- The sailboat that scouts new land

The money is a tool.
The communities are the purpose.

CrowdWare DAO is not yet founded, because that will cost about 100.000 CHF for notars and the like.

## books.crowdware.info

The book you are reading is available free at books.crowdware.info.

Because knowledge should not be behind a paywall.
Because the junior developer in Lagos deserves the same access as the senior developer in Berlin.
Because the pioneer's job is to leave the path visible – and visible means accessible.

For those who want a physical copy – Amazon.
For those who want to support the work – the commercial license.
For those who have nothing – the website.

All three versions contain exactly the same content.

No premium tier. No exclusive insights. No paywalled chapters.

The same book. Three prices. Zero, a little, and enough to fund the next one.

## Distribution Without Gatekeepers

Traditional publishing requires:
- An agent
- A publisher
- A marketing budget
- A platform
- Months of waiting

books.crowdware.info requires:
- A Markdown file
- A cover image
- Five minutes

This is Forge eating its own cooking again.

The platform that removes barriers between ideas and software also removes barriers between authors and readers.

Write it. Publish it. Done.

The gatekeepers are optional.

## The Real World

I want to be honest about something.

Building in the open, with transparent licenses and DAO governance and community ownership – this is not the path of least resistance.

Investors prefer control.
Corporations prefer proprietary.
The market prefers what it already knows.

But the market is not always right.

Linux was not the path of least resistance.
Wikipedia was not the path of least resistance.
The entire open source ecosystem that powers the modern internet was not the path of least resistance.

And yet here we are.

The path of least resistance leads to where everyone else already is.

The other path leads somewhere new.

I have always taken the other path.
Not because I enjoy difficulty.
Because the destination is worth it.
